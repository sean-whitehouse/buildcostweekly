Build a Simple Bid Template That Protects Your Margin

Guides › Estimating · Evergreen — reviewed July 2026

A bid template does two jobs: it stops you from forgetting costs, and it stops the customer from expanding the job for free. One page covers both. Build it once, reuse it forever.

The structure

  1. Scope of work — plain sentences, one per line, each starting with a verb. "Demo existing deck and haul away. Frame new 12×16 deck per sketch. Install composite decking and railing." If it's not written here, it's not in the price.
  2. Materials — from your takeoff, with waste included (see waste factors), plus delivery, fasteners/consumables, and tax.
  3. Labor — crew-hours × fully loaded rate. On the customer's copy this can be one line; on your copy keep the task breakdown so you can compare estimate vs. actual afterward.
  4. Overhead & margin — on your internal copy, always as explicit lines. Never let them live inside "labor" where a discount conversation can eat them.
  5. Exclusions — the section that saves marriages and margins.
  6. Terms — validity window, payment schedule, change-order rule.

The exclusions most small operators forget

  • Permit fees and drawings (or state who carries them)
  • Rock, unsuitable soils, or concealed conditions once opened up
  • Rot/mold/asbestos discovered during demo
  • Utility relocation, engineering, and survey
  • Painting/finishing beyond what's stated
  • Landscape repair beyond rough grade

Three sentences that protect you from price moves

"This price is valid for 21 days from the date above."

"Material prices are based on supplier quotes current as of this date; increases beyond 5% at time of order will be passed through at cost with documentation."

"Changes to the scope above are priced and signed as written change orders before the work is done."

The validity window matters more than most people think — lumber has moved several percent in a month plenty of times (the weekly snapshot tracks exactly this). An open-ended bid is a free option you're giving away. Whether an escalation clause is enforceable varies by state and contract — have a local attorney sanity-check your terms once; it's cheap insurance.

Keep two versions

The customer copy shows scope, a clean price (lump sum or a few sections), exclusions, and terms. The internal copy holds the full math — quantities, rates, overhead, margin. Same template, two tabs. When a job ends, spend ten minutes writing actuals next to estimates. That feedback loop is what makes your next bid sharper, and it's the part almost nobody does.
