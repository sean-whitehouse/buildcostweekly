How to Estimate a Small Residential Job (Without Losing Your Shirt)

Guides › Estimating · Evergreen — reviewed July 2026

Most small-job estimates don't fail because the math was wrong. They fail because whole categories of cost never made it onto the page. Here's the five-part structure that keeps every cost visible.

1. Quantities first, prices last

Do the takeoff before you look at a single price. Walk the scope and list every quantity: square feet of drywall, linear feet of plate, sheet count, yards of concrete, door and window counts. When you price too early, you anchor on a total and unconsciously make the quantities fit it.

Write quantities in the units your supplier sells, not the units on the plans. Plans give you square feet; the yard sells you sheets, sticks, and yards.

2. Materials — with waste and delivery

Price the takeoff, then add a per-material waste factor (framing lumber isn't tile — see our waste factors guide). Then add the costs that hide outside the unit price:

  • Delivery fees — often $75–$150 per drop, and small jobs need multiple drops
  • Fasteners, adhesives, and consumables — commonly 3–5% of rough material cost
  • Sales tax, if your quote basis doesn't include it

3. Labor — hours, not vibes

Estimate labor in crew-hours per task, then multiply by a fully loaded rate: wages plus payroll taxes, workers' comp, and insurance. A $30/hr carpenter typically costs $40–$45/hr loaded. If you bid your own time, pay yourself the same loaded rate — "free" owner labor is how profitable jobs turn into charity.

4. Overhead — the silent killer

Truck, fuel, tools, phone, insurance, software, the hours you spend quoting jobs you don't win: it all has to be carried by the jobs you do win. Total your annual overhead, divide by your realistic billable field hours, and add that number to every labor hour. For many one-to-three-person outfits it lands between $8 and $20 per hour. If you've never done this exercise, do it once — it explains a lot of vanished profit.

5. Margin — added on purpose, not left over

Profit is a line item, not whatever's left after surprises. Decide your target margin (10–20% net is a common small-operator range) and apply it as a markup on the full cost — materials, labor, and overhead. And know the difference: a 20% markup is not a 20% margin. Markup is on cost; margin is on price. To net 20% of the sale price you need to mark costs up 25%.

The 60-second sanity check

Before the number goes out the door: Did every scope item get a quantity? Does every quantity have waste? Is labor at loaded rates? Is overhead in there? Is margin a line, not a leftover? Five yeses — send it.
