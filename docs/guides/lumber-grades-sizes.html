Lumber Grades & Sizes, Explained in Plain English

Guides › Buying materials · Evergreen — reviewed July 2026

Two things confuse every new builder at the lumberyard: the board isn't the size it's called, and the stamp on it reads like a license plate. Both take five minutes to learn and save real money.

Nominal vs. actual: the 2×4 is not 2"×4"

Dimensional lumber is named by its rough-sawn size before drying and planing. What you buy is smaller:

NominalActual (dry)
2×41½" × 3½"
2×61½" × 5½"
2×81½" × 7¼"
2×101½" × 9¼"
2×121½" × 11¼"
1×6 (board)¾" × 5½"

It matters most when you're matching old framing — a 1950s "true" 2×4 is fatter than today's — and when a design counts on actual dimensions for depth of a beam or a shelf.

Reading the grade stamp

A typical stamp tells you five things: the grading agency, the mill number, the grade, the species (or species group), and the moisture condition. The two you'll actually use:

  • Grade — for framing lumber the common ladder is Select Structural, then No. 1, No. 2, No. 3, and Stud grade. Strength and appearance step down as the number goes up. Most residential framing is specified as No. 2 or better — knots allowed, structurally sound.
  • Moisture — "KD" or "KD-HT" (kiln dried), "S-DRY" (surfaced dry, ≤19% moisture), or "S-GRN" (green, >19%). Green lumber is cheaper and will shrink and move as it dries in place. Fine for some uses; a callback machine in others.

Species groups matter for strength: SPF (spruce-pine-fir), Doug Fir-Larch, Hem-Fir, and Southern Yellow Pine are the big four in North America. If an engineer specified the species on a beam or header, it's not a suggestion.

When to pay for better

  • Worth it: long joists and rafters near their span limit (fewer weakening knots), stair stringers, anything visible that gets clear finish, plates and studs for walls that must stay dead straight (kitchens with full-height cabinets, long tiled walls).
  • Not worth it: blocking, short cripples, temporary bracing, and most wall framing that will be buried in drywall. Buy No. 2/Stud grade and cull the worst sticks for blocking.
  • The cheap move that beats grade: hand-pick at the yard when they let you, and sight down every stick. A straight No. 2 beats a twisted No. 1 every day of the week.

Pressure-treated is its own world

Treated lumber is graded the same way but sold wet and heavy — expect shrinkage and movement. Use it where code requires (ground contact, sill plates on concrete) and check the treatment level stamp: "ground contact" and "above ground" are different products.
