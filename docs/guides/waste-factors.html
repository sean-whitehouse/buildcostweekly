Waste Factors: The Numbers Pros Actually Use

Guides › Estimating · Evergreen — reviewed July 2026

"Add 10% for waste" is the most repeated rule of thumb in construction — and it's wrong in both directions. Ten percent on a simple rectangular deck is money thrown away; ten percent on a herringbone tile floor means a second trip to the store mid-job. Waste is a function of material and geometry, so estimate it that way.

Baseline factors by material

MaterialTypical rangeDrives it up
Framing lumber5–10%Non-standard lengths, lots of blocking, culling bowed sticks
Sheathing / OSB / plywood5–10%Angled walls, gable cuts, small rooms
Drywall10–15%High ceilings, many openings, stairwells
Asphalt shingles10–15%Hips, valleys, dormers (cut-up roofs run to 20%)
Plank flooring (straight lay)7–10%Wide rooms with short walls, patterns, borders
Tile (straight lay)10%Diagonal +5%, herringbone/patterns +15%, large-format in small rooms
Concrete (ready-mix)5–10%Sub-base irregularity, forms out of square — round up to the next quarter yard regardless
Siding10%Gables, many corners, mitered details

Three adjustments worth making

  • Complex geometry beats the table. Count cuts, not just area. A cut-up roof or an angled room takes the top of the range plus.
  • Dye lots and discontinuation. For tile and flooring, order the overage with the main order and keep a box for repairs. Matching a dye lot a year later is a lottery ticket.
  • Who's cutting matters. A rookie crew wastes more than a crew that's run the same detail fifty times. Estimate for the crew you have.

Waste is also theft and weather

On jobs longer than a couple of weeks, some "waste" is really loss: rained-on drywall, walked-off 2×4s, the sheet that became a mud board. Secure storage and tarps are cheaper than the replacement order and the schedule slip.
