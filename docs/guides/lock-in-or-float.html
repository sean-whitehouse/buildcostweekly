Lock In Material Prices or Float Them? A Simple Decision Rule

Guides › Buying materials · Evergreen — reviewed July 2026

You bid a job in July. It starts in October. Do you protect today's material prices, or take your chances? Small operators tend to answer this by gut. Here's a rule you can actually apply.

The rule: time × share × direction

Ask three questions:

  1. Time gap. How long between pricing the job and ordering the material? Under 30 days, price risk is usually noise — skip the rest of this article. Over 60 days, keep reading.
  2. Material share. What fraction of the contract is the volatile material? A framing package on a garage build might be 30% of contract value; on a bathroom remodel, lumber is a rounding error. Protect the big shares, ignore the small ones.
  3. Direction. What has the market done over the last quarter? You don't need to predict — just don't bid against a visible trend. (This is exactly what the weekly snapshot is for.)

If the gap is 60+ days and the material is a double-digit share of the contract and the trend is up or choppy — protect the price. Otherwise float it and don't overthink.

Four ways to protect a price, cheapest first

  • Written quote with a hold period. Many lumberyards will hold a quoted package price 15–30 days in writing, sometimes longer for good accounts. Free, and most small operators never ask.
  • Escalation clause in the contract. Pass increases beyond a threshold (commonly 5%) through to the customer at documented cost. Fair both ways if you also pass through decreases. Check enforceability in your state.
  • Contingency in the bid. Add 3–5% to the volatile package. Simple, invisible, but it can price you out of tight competitive bids.
  • Buy early and store. Only when you have dry, secure storage and the cash — and remember stored lumber can twist, and stored material is capital sitting under a tarp.

What not to do

Don't try to time the market on a single job — buying early because "prices always spike in spring" is trading, not building. And never leave a fixed-price bid open-ended while material markets move underneath it; a validity window (see the bid template) costs one sentence.

A worked example

Detached garage, $60K contract, framing package $18K (30%), bid in July for an October start (90 days), and the snapshot shows lumber up 6.5% year-over-year and drifting higher. That's yes-yes-yes: ask the yard for a 30-day written hold, put a 5% escalation clause in the contract for the gap the hold doesn't cover, and sleep fine.
