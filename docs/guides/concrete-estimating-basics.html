Concrete Estimating Basics for Small Jobs

Guides › Estimating · Evergreen — reviewed July 2026

To estimate ready-mix concrete, convert your slab's length, width, and thickness to feet, multiply them together, divide by 27 to get cubic yards, then add 5–10% waste and round up to the next quarter yard. Pick the PSI your application and local code call for — commonly 3,000 for footings and 3,500–4,000 for driveways — rather than taking whatever the plant defaults to.

Concrete is one of the few materials you can't top off with a quick run to the store. Order short and you're either cold-jointing a slab or paying for a second truck plus a delivery minimum; order long and you're paying for mud that goes down the storm drain. The math isn't hard — the discipline is ordering to it honestly.

Step 1: Get to cubic yards

Ready-mix is sold by the cubic yard, and one cubic yard is 27 cubic feet. For a flat pour, the formula is:

Yards = (length ft × width ft × thickness ft) ÷ 27

The trap is thickness: it's in feet, not inches. Convert first — 4" is 0.33 ft, 6" is 0.5 ft, 8" is 0.67 ft.

Slab thicknessSq ft per cubic yardCommon use
4"81 sq ftSidewalks, patios, residential floors
5"65 sq ftLight driveways
6"54 sq ftDriveways, garage slabs, light-truck traffic
8"40 sq ftHeavy loads, some footings

Quick check for a slab: divide your square footage by the number above. A 20×24 (480 sq ft) driveway at 6" is 480 ÷ 54 ≈ 8.9 yards before waste.

Step 2: Add the right waste — then round up to the truck

  • Add 5–10% for waste — spillage, an out-of-square form, a sub-base that dips low in spots. Take the high end when the ground is rough or the forms are hand-built.
  • Round up to the next quarter yard regardless. Plants batch in quarter-yard steps, and being a hair short is far more expensive than being a hair long.
  • Know the short-load fee. Most plants charge a per-yard surcharge under a minimum (often 3–5 yards). A tiny pour can cost nearly the same as a bigger one — worth knowing before you quote.

Step 3: Pick the PSI

PSI is the concrete's compressive strength. Higher isn't automatically better — it's what the application needs. Confirm against your local code and any engineer's spec; these are typical residential ranges:

ApplicationTypical PSI
Interior slab / basement floor (not freeze-exposed)2,500–3,000
Footings & foundation walls3,000
Driveways, garage slabs, patios3,500–4,000
Exterior flatwork in freeze-thaw climates4,000+ with air entrainment

In freeze-thaw country, the air entrainment (tiny bubbles that give freezing water room to expand) matters as much as the PSI — ask for it on any exterior pour.

Step 4: The ordering call

  • Give the plant the real details: yardage, PSI, air entrainment yes/no, slump (how wet — 4" is a common default), and whether you need pump mix or fiber. Each affects price and workability.
  • Book the window, not just the day. Confirm your pour time and how many trucks; a big slab that outruns one truck needs them spaced so you're not cold-jointing while you wait.
  • Watch the wash-out and wait time. Trucks include a set number of unloading minutes; going over racks up demurrage. Have your crew, tools, and finishers ready before the truck rolls in.

The one that bites small operators

Ordering exactly to the calculated number with no cushion. A form that's 1/2" deep across a big slab, or a sub-base graded a touch low, quietly adds yardage — and you find out when the truck runs dry three feet from the end. Carry the waste factor, round up, and keep a bag or two of high-early patch mix on site for the genuinely small misses.
