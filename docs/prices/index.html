What this means for your bid

Direction: firmer — last week's split has closed, and it closed upward. Lumber futures reversed: $635, up about 2.4% from last week's snapshot and back near the one-year high around $640. The month is now essentially flat (−0.2%) rather than falling, though futures are still about 4.9% below a year ago. The cash market — the Madison's framing composite, what your yard actually buys from — has no new reading this week (the July 10 figure of $547 is still the latest published), and it was up 4.4% on the month. Supply is doing the work: US sawmill capacity is down roughly 6% year-over-year, and tariff pressure on Canadian imports plus redirected export flows are keeping physical lumber tight. Southern Pine dimensional items led July's move, with 2x12 up double digits. Translation: the "relief is coming" read from last week has weakened — treat framing as firm until the next composite print says otherwise.

  • Bidding a job that starts within 30 days? Same rule, more urgency: bid off a current written yard quote, and get it in writing this week rather than next. With futures back near their one-year high, a quote you pulled two weeks ago may already be stale.
  • Bidding now for a start 60+ days out? The sideways-to-down cushion is gone for now. Consider getting a written supplier quote with a hold period and carrying a somewhat wider framing contingency than you would in a flat market — and ask your yard specifically about Southern Pine items, which moved hardest this month.
  • Buying for a job already under contract? Still no panic-buy case — the futures move is a rebound to the top of the recent range, not a breakout, and the year-over-year number is still negative. If you have material you know you'll need and storage for it, there's a reasonable argument for buying earlier in your normal schedule rather than later.

FAQ

How often are these prices updated?

Every week. The date at the top of the page ("Week of...") is the week this snapshot covers.

Where do these numbers come from?

Two sources, both linked in the Sources section above: Trading Economics for lumber futures, and the NAHB/Madison's Lumber Price Index for the framing lumber composite. Both are dated so you can check the primary source yourself.

Why do the futures price and the composite price sometimes move in different directions?

Futures are traders' forward bet on where lumber is headed; the Madison's composite tracks what's actually trading in the cash market right now. They usually move together but can diverge for weeks at a time — when they disagree, the "What this means for your bid" callout says so.

Will my local lumberyard price match this exactly?

No. These are wholesale indexes, not retail prices — your yard's counter price will differ and typically lags these moves by 2–6 weeks. Always confirm with a written supplier quote before you bid; don't bid off the index number.

What materials besides lumber are tracked?

Right now, just framing lumber (futures and the Madison's composite). OSB/panel, drywall, rebar/steel, and ready-mix concrete are being added as reliable free sources are verified — see the note above if there's one you want prioritized.
