Estimating Tools for Small Builders: From Notepad to Software

Tools · Reviewed July 2026

This page may contain affiliate links — if you buy through one, we may earn a small commission at no cost to you. Picks are made on merit first. Full disclosure.

Estimating software companies would like you to believe you're losing jobs because you don't have their product. Usually you're losing margin because of missing line items, not missing software (fix that first with the bid template). Here's what's actually worth using at each stage.

Stage 1 — Under ~10 bids a year: a spreadsheet, done properly

Excel or Google Sheets is genuinely the right tool at this volume, if you build it once with structure: a takeoff tab, a rates tab (loaded labor rates, current material prices, waste factors), and a bid tab that reads from both. The discipline is updating the rates tab — stale prices are the spreadsheet's only real weakness, and it's exactly why the weekly snapshot exists.

  • Cost: $0–$7/month.
  • Upgrade trigger: you're retyping the same assemblies every bid, or two people need to edit at once.

Stage 2 — Takeoff help: measure off plans and photos

The first genuinely worthwhile purchase isn't estimating software — it's takeoff tooling that gets quantities off PDFs faster and more accurately than scaling with a ruler:

  • A digital takeoff app for measuring lengths/areas/counts directly on plan PDFs. Free tiers exist and are enough for small jobs.
  • A laser distance measurer (~$60–$150) for existing-conditions work — remodel takeoffs go twice as fast, and interior measurements stop being two-person jobs. Our pick: [PRODUCT — affiliate link pending program approval].

Stage 3 — 25+ bids a year or multiple crews: paid software earns its keep

At this volume, assembly libraries (type "deck, composite, 12×16" and get a costed package), integrated proposals, and change-order tracking start paying for their subscription in hours saved and items not forgotten. Expect $50–$200/month for small-builder tiers. Two honest warnings:

  • The default cost databases are national averages — they are starting points, not bids. Your local labor market and your yard's prices are the truth.
  • The switching cost is real. Trial with one live bid before you migrate everything.

The tool that beats all of these

A job-cost history. Whatever you estimate with, record actual hours and actual material costs when the job closes. Ten jobs of estimate-vs-actual data will improve your bidding more than any subscription. It's a 10-minute habit, and almost nobody does it.
